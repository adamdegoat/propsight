What it's like

Holland Village is where people pay to live next to the dining, the Circle Line and one-north, in a low-rise pocket of prime District 10.

Holland Village sits in prime District 10, a low-rise pocket of restaurants, bars and shops built around Lorong Mambong and the Holland Village MRT on the Circle Line. It is one of the places where the planning map fails you: the village straddles the Queenstown and Bukit Timah planning areas, so a search by planning area alone splits a neighbourhood that has always read as one place. The housing is led by private stock, condominiums and the landed streets of Chip Bee Gardens, with a small pocket of HDB flats along Holland Drive, Holland Close and Holland Avenue. That mix is why a $992k four-room flat and a landed home several times that price can sit a few minutes apart.

What people buy or rent into is the location and the way of life. The dining strip on Lorong Mambong, the wet market and hawker stalls at Holland Village Market, and the cafes and galleries of Chip Bee Gardens have drawn expats, professionals and academics for decades, helped by how close it sits to the National University of Singapore and the research and tech campuses of one-north and Buona Vista next door. The new One Holland Village mixed development added apartments, shops and a public square to the old core. Henry Park Primary and other well-regarded schools are within reach, so families settle here too, not only singles and couples.

Getting around is straightforward. The Circle Line at Holland Village loops you to the CBD, the east and the Botanic Gardens interchange without going through town, and Holland Road and the expressways are close for drivers. The honest catch is the price of entry. This is prime, mostly freehold ground, so condos run to a $3.06m median and the landed homes are well beyond that, while rental yields sit around 2.7%, thin by Singapore standards. Some of the condo stock is older, the dining strip gets busy and parking-tight on weekends, and the HDB supply is small, so the cheaper way in is limited to a handful of flats along Holland Drive.

Who it suitsExpats, professionals and academics who want a low-rise, walkable, dining-led spot near town and one-north, buying prime condos or landed homes or renting. The cheaper HDB pool is small, limited to the Holland Drive flats.

The honest view

What works

  • A genuinely central, low-rise address with the dining, the market and the Circle Line all on foot, and one-north and NUS next door.
  • Strong rental demand from expats, professionals and academics, so tenanted homes tend to let quickly.
  • Well-regarded schools such as Henry Park Primary within reach, and the new One Holland Village adds shops, homes and a public square to the old core.

Worth weighing

  • Expensive to buy into, with a condo median around $3.06m and landed homes well beyond, most of it prime freehold.
  • Yields are thin, roughly 2.7% gross on a condo, because this is bought to live in rather than to rent out.
  • Some condo stock is older, the dining strip gets crowded and parking-tight on weekends, and the HDB supply is small, limited to the Holland Drive flats.
