Sengkang

A young north-east town built around the North-East Line and its own LRT loops, with Compass One and Sengkang Grand Mall at the interchange and the river park a short walk away.

What it's like

Sengkang is a new-build family town where most of the housing, and most of the residents, are still on the younger side.

Sengkang went from farmland and prawn ponds to a full HDB town in barely two decades, most of it built out from the late 1990s through the 2000s. That shows in the feel of the place. The blocks are newer and taller than the older estates, the void decks are tidier, and the town centre is a single clean cluster: Compass One and Sengkang Grand Mall sit right on top of the interchange, with the bus interchange, the polyclinic and the hawker centre all in the same few minutes' walk. The Punggol and Sungei Serangoon waterways wrap the edge of the town, and Sengkang Riverside Park with its floating wetland gives it a genuine bit of green and water rather than just grass verges.

The housing is mostly newer BTO flats plus a run of executive condos and private launches like Sengkang Grand Residences, The Quartz and Riversound Residence. Four-room resale flats sit around $645k, which for a flat with a long lease left is on the affordable side by island standards, and the town skews heavily toward young families: first-time buyers who balloted for a BTO, upgraders moving out of their parents' estates, and plenty of prams and childcare centres. It is a settled young-family town, not a nightlife or hipster one, and it does not pretend otherwise.

Transport is the double-edged part. The North-East Line runs straight through Sengkang interchange, and the two Sengkang LRT loops fan out to feed the further blocks, so most homes are a reasonable walk or one LRT stop from the train. The honest catch is the geography: this is the far north-east, so the ride into the Raffles Place and Shenton Way core runs around 40 minutes on a good day, and the LRT picks up its share of grumbles when it is packed or when a fault backs up the loop. If your work and life stay in the north-east it is easy. If you commute into town daily, you feel the distance.

Who it suitsYoung families who want a newer flat with a long lease, everything walkable around the interchange, and don't mind sitting at the far north-east edge.

The honest view

What works

  • The flats are newer with long leases left, and a four-room still grosses close to 6.0% in rent.
  • The town centre is compact and walkable: Compass One, Sengkang Grand Mall, the polyclinic, hawker centre and interchange all in one cluster.
  • Real waterfront green at Sengkang Riverside Park and the floating wetland, not just grass verges.

Worth weighing

  • It sits at the far north-east, so the daily commute into the central business district is a long one, around 40 minutes.
  • The Sengkang LRT loops take their share of reliability grumbles, especially at peak or when a fault backs up the line.
  • Condos are a big jump in price for modest yields near 3.5%, and parts of the town still feel like they are maturing.
