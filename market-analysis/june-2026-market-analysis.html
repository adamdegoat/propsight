Singapore homes are holding near record prices, but the market has quietly split. Mature-estate HDB and the suburbs are pulling ahead, while prime private and a handful of heartland towns have gone cold.

A monthly read on the whole market, from real transacted prices. Free.

The month in brief
Private

Resale prices are plateauing at a high level, not falling. Median psf sits near $1,753, essentially flat on the month and about +3.8% over the year. Deal flow is steady at roughly 1,100 to 1,300 a month.

Segments

The suburbs (OCR) and city fringe (RCR) are firming, on 3-month momentum of +1.4% and +0.6%. Core Central prime has slipped -2.4%. The premium you pay to be central is compressing.

HDB

Resale looks flat on the surface (~$605 psf, +0.3% over the year), but that average hides a wide split. Mature, central towns are surging while a few heartland estates ease.

Rental

Private rents sit near $4,300/mo for a 3.6% gross yield; HDB near $3,200/mo for 6.1%. For cash yield, HDB is still the stronger landlord play by a wide margin.

New launches

Buyers are paying $2,361 to $5,947 psf for new suburban and fringe projects, well above the $1,523 psf you'd pay resale in the same suburbs. The gap between new and old has rarely been wider.

Policy

No housing changes in Budget 2026. Every cooling measure stays in force: ABSD, TDSR, and the tighter Seller's Stamp Duty from July 2025. Nothing has moved to lower the cost of entry.

Private resale has settled into a plateau. The median stands near $1,753 psf, drifting +0.6% on the last full month and holding +3.8% higher than a year ago. This is a market that has stopped climbing quickly but shows no sign of breaking. Prices are firm, volumes are normal, and there is no distress on the tape.

The more interesting story is underneath the average. The suburbs (OCR, $1,523 psf) and the city fringe (RCR, $1,962 psf) are the ones still nudging up, on 3-month momentum of +1.4% and +0.6%. Core Central (CCR, $2,203 psf), the prime districts, has softened -2.4%. Prime is where the foreigner 60% ABSD bites hardest, and it shows. The long-running story of the heartland closing the gap on the centre is still very much intact.

What this means for buyers

Resale is where the value sits, the new-launch premium is near its widest. Prioritise location and remaining lease over newness; those are what held their price this cycle.

New-launch premium over resale

The new-launch market is running hot and expensive. The best-sellers this cycle, Parc Clematis, Parktown Residence, Springleaf Residence are clearing at $2,361 to $3,051 psf, mostly in the OCR and RCR. Set that against the $1,523 psf OCR resale median and the primary-to-resale premium is stark: new stock is pricing 40% to 60% above comparable resale in the same region.

Some of that gap is real (brand-new, smaller efficient layouts, full facilities); a lot of it is the developer's land cost from a tight Government Land Sales market being passed straight to the buyer. For anyone not wedded to buying new, resale is where the value has quietly moved.

On paper HDB resale is flat, a ~$605 psf median and +0.3% over the year. In reality it has split into two markets. Mature, central and well-located towns are surging on 3-month momentum: Marine Parade +11.0%, Toa Payoh +10.6%, Central Area +9.5% and Bukit Merah +6.4%. This is where the million-dollar flats keep printing.

At the other end, a handful of towns have cooled hard: Geylang -7.5%, Kallang/Whampoa -4.8%, Ang Mo Kio -4.0%. Some of that is mix (which blocks happened to transact), but the direction is clear. Buyers are paying up for location and lease, and getting choosier everywhere else. Across flat types, a 4-room now runs a $630k median, a 5-room $740k, and an Executive $910k.

What this means for upgraders

The private plateau plus softer prime pricing narrows the gap from a strong HDB sale to a private purchase, the classic upgrader window. Sell into strength, buy where prime has eased.

Rents off the boil, HDB still the yield play

Rents have come off the boil but stayed high. Private lets sit near $4,300 a month, a gross yield of about 3.6% once you divide by today's prices. HDB rents near $3,200 a month throw off roughly 6.1%, which is the number that should make any yield-focused landlord look twice.

The 2024 to 2025 completion wave added supply and expat demand has cooled, so the pressure is off tenants for the first time in a while. For landlords, the private-versus-HDB yield gap is the headline: private pays in appreciation and prestige, HDB pays the rent.

What this means for landlords

Yields have compressed but HDB still pays far better cash than private (~6% vs ~3.6%). Expect flat-to-softer rents into 2027 as completions and MOP supply arrive; lock good tenants rather than chase the top dollar.

Supply & en bloc

The pipeline behind 2027 and 2028 prices

Collective sales are simmering again. Loyang Valley went for $880.00m in April, and a tight land-sales pipeline is pushing developers back toward en bloc to restock. That is bullish for owners in ageing estates on large plots, and a slow tailwind for prices two to three years out as replacement supply gets priced at today's land cost.

On the HDB side, a heavy wave of flats is reaching their 5-year MOP and becoming sellable, adding more supply into the resale pool over the next year, concentrated in the newer towns. That is the natural pressure valve on the mature-estate surge.

Rates & policy

Cooling measures intact, rates in charge

The policy backdrop is unchanged and, for buyers, unforgiving. Budget 2026 made no housing moves. ABSD stands at 20% on a second home for citizens and 60% for foreigners; the Seller's Stamp Duty tightened in July 2025 to a four-year holding window, which keeps flippers out and rewards holders. TDSR caps borrowing at 55% of income and banks stress-test around 4%.

Read together, the measures do exactly what they were built to do: they cap speculative demand and reward long holders. Nothing here is about to unlock a wave of cheap entry, so the market's direction will be set by rates and supply, not policy loosening.

The month ahead

Outlook

Into the second half of 2026, base case: prices grind sideways to slightly up rather than break in either direction. The suburbs and mature-estate HDB keep their edge; prime private stays soft until foreign demand or the ABSD math changes. Watch three things: the pace of new launches (a thin pipeline supports resale), the MOP supply wave hitting HDB, and borrowing costs, the single biggest swing factor for affordability.

The honest risks cut both ways. Faster rate cuts could reignite prices and pull en-bloc forward; a rate reversal or a demand shock would expose the suburban pockets that have run hardest. But distress is not on this data. Volumes are healthy and pricing is orderly.

Our call

Our call: a firm, unexciting market for the rest of 2026. Flat to up low single digits on private, flat on HDB in aggregate, with the real money made on location and lease, not on the tide. The value trade is resale over new, and mature-estate HDB over the average.
