The month in brief
Private

Private resale prices sit at a median $1,753 psf, up 0.6% on the month and 3.8% on the year, with a typical deal around $1.65m. Momentum is steady.

Segments

Suburbs (OCR) lead at $1,523 psf, up 1.4% over three months, and the city-fringe (RCR) holds at $1,962 psf, up 0.6%. Prime (CCR) is the outlier at $2,203 psf, down 2.4%, so the premium for a central address is thinning.

HDB

In the aggregate flats are flat, with the resale median at $605 psf and 0.3% higher on the year, but the map is uneven. Marine Parade (+11.0%), Toa Payoh (+10.6%), the Central Area (+9.5%) and Bukit Merah (+6.4%) are pulling ahead, while Geylang (-7.5%), Kallang/Whampoa (-4.8%), Ang Mo Kio (-4.0%) and Bukit Panjang (-3.3%) are cooling. A wave of flats reaching their 5-year MOP over the next year should add resale supply.

Rental

A private home rents for a median $4,300 a month on a 3.6% gross yield, while an HDB flat fetches $3,200 on a 6.1% yield. For income, the numbers favour the heartland.

New launches

Buyers are paying up for new OCR launches well above the $1,523 psf suburban resale benchmark, from Tengah Garden Residences at $2,113 psf and Springleaf Residence at $2,169 psf to Parktown Residence at $2,361 psf and Chuan Park at $2,598 psf. In the RCR, The Orie ($2,723 psf) and One Marina Gardens ($2,957 psf) show how wide the gap to resale has grown.

Policy

Nothing changed, and Budget 2026 left housing alone. ABSD stays at 20% on a citizen's second home and 60% for foreigners, Seller's Stamp Duty runs on a 4-year window (16% if you sell within a year), TDSR is capped at 55% and you can borrow up to 75% on a first bank loan.

Private resale prices are flattening out. The median now sits at $1,753 psf, up just 0.6% over the month and 3.8% over the year, with the typical deal closing at about $1.65m. Across the past 18 months the market has logged 22,196 transactions, so this is a broad reading, not a thin one swinging on a handful of sales. The latest month is still preliminary, but the headline is calm.

That calm hides a clear split by location. Suburban homes in the OCR, at $1,523 psf, are still climbing, up 1.4% over the last three months. The city fringe RCR, at $1,962 psf, has slowed to 0.6%. Prime CCR, at $2,203 psf, has gone backwards, down 2.4%. The money is chasing the mass market and stepping back from the top end, the reverse of how most people picture a recovery.

What this means for buyers

Hunt in the OCR suburbs near the $1,523 psf resale benchmark where momentum is strongest, and do not overpay in prime CCR while it is still sliding 2.4%.

New-launch premium over resale

New launches are selling at prices that make resale look cheap. In the suburbs, where existing homes trade around $1,523 psf, buyers are paying $2,113 psf at Tengah Garden Residences, $2,169 at Springleaf Residence, $2,361 at Parktown Residence and $2,598 at Chuan Park. Move to the city fringe and the numbers climb again, to $2,723 psf at The Orie and $2,957 at One Marina Gardens. These are the best sellers, so this is not a few overpriced units sitting unsold.

What that premium buys you is a new building, a fresh lease and no near-term repair bills. What it costs you is most of the upside on day one. Paying $2,113 psf or more in a suburb where resale sits at $1,523 psf means the wider market has to climb a long way before you recover the gap over the going rate. For buyers who care about value, a well-located resale unit leaves far more room for the price to work in their favour.

On paper the HDB resale market is barely moving. The overall median is $605 psf, up just 0.3% over the year, and the flat medians look steady: $446k for a 3-room, $630k for a 4-room, $740k for a 5-room and $910k for an executive. Read only that line and you would think nothing much is happening.

Underneath, the towns are pulling apart. Over the last three months Marine Parade is up 11.0%, Toa Payoh 10.6%, Central Area 9.5% and Bukit Merah 6.4%, while Geylang has fallen 7.5%, Kallang/Whampoa 4.8%, Ang Mo Kio 4.0% and Bukit Panjang 3.3%. Buyers are paying up for central, well-connected estates and walking away from the rest, and with a wave of flats reaching their five-year minimum occupation period over the next year, the cooling estates are the ones most likely to feel the extra supply.

Rental & yield

Rents off the boil, HDB still the yield play

The heat has come out of the rental market. Private homes now let for a median $4,300 a month, and at today's prices that works out to a gross yield of 3.6%. After the surge of the past few years, tenants have some footing back: more completed units, more choice, and less willingness to chase asking rents higher. Landlords who bought at recent prices are finding the sums tighter, and holding out for a top rent increasingly means holding out for an empty flat.

For pure income, HDB remains the stronger play. A flat rents for a median $3,200 a month, but because the entry price is so much lower, the gross yield lands at 6.1%, ahead of the 3.6% on private. If you are buying to rent out rather than to live in, the maths favours the flat by a wide margin. The trade-off is a smaller tenant pool and the rules around subletting, but on yield alone the flat wins.

What this means for landlords

Favour HDB for its 6.1% yield over private's 3.6%, and price to fill quickly because rents have come off the boil and empty months cost more than a lower rent.

Supply & en bloc

The pipeline behind 2027 and 2028 prices

The collective sale market has woken up. Three deals stand out: Chuan Park at $890.00m, Loyang Valley at $880.00m, and Thiam Siew Avenue at $815.00m. Each of these sites gets torn down and rebuilt into new launches, and every owner who cashes out becomes a buyer hunting for a replacement home. That is why en bloc waves tend to firm up resale prices in the near term, even as they add to future supply.

The bigger story is the flats. A wave of HDB homes reaches their 5-year minimum occupation period over the coming year, which frees a fresh stream of owners to list. More resale supply usually caps how fast prices can run. Put the two together, the en bloc rebuilds and the MOP flats, and you get the pipeline that shapes 2027 and 2028: new completions landing just as more owners are cleared to sell. Prices in those years will lean less on today's demand and more on how cleanly all that supply gets absorbed.

Rates & policy

Cooling measures intact, rates in charge

None of the rules changed. Budget 2026 left housing alone, so the cooling framework stands as it was. Additional Buyer's Stamp Duty is still 20% on a citizen's second home and 60% for foreigners, which keeps most speculative and overseas money on the sidelines. The Seller's Stamp Duty was tightened last July to a 4-year holding window, with a 16% charge if you sell within the first year, so flipping is expensive and slow. Borrowing stays capped by the 55% total debt servicing ratio and a 75% loan-to-value limit on a first bank loan.

With the policy dial frozen, interest rates are what move this market now. The stamp duties set the ceiling on how much churn is possible, but the cost of a mortgage decides how much a buyer can put on the table. When rates ease, budgets stretch and prices firm; when they climb, that same 55% limit bites harder and demand thins out. For your signal over the next few months, watch the rate cycle, not the rule book.

The month ahead

Outlook

Base case for the month ahead is more of the same slow grind higher. Private resale is up 3.8% over the year and just 0.6% on the month, a steady climb rather than a boom. The suburbs are doing the heavy lifting, with OCR up 1.4% over three months, while prime CCR is down 2.4% as high-end demand stays soft. Expect the broad median, now $1,753 psf, to keep inching up at that pace, with the value tiers leading and the top end lagging.

The risks cut both ways. On the upside, if rates fall faster than expected the sidelined buyers come back and the suburbs could run hotter than the recent 1.4%. On the downside, the prime market's 2.4% slide is a reminder that the top end can turn first, and the coming supply of MOP flats and en bloc rebuilds could dent sentiment if it lands all at once. The most likely path is neither extreme: a market that keeps grinding sideways to slightly up while everyone waits on the rate cycle.

Our call

Our call: a slow grind higher led by the suburbs, with HDB the better yield and prime the weak spot, so buy the value tiers and wait on the rate cycle before you stretch.
