How much do you really need to buy an HDB flat?

about 6 min read

Most first-time buyers ask "how much is the flat?" The more useful question is "how much do I actually need to find, and in what form?" Here is the honest breakdown, piece by piece.

The price on the listing is only the start. What really matters is how much cash you need on the day, how much can come from CPF, and what the bank will lend you against your income. Get those three straight and the rest is detail. Below is every piece that makes up the cost, in plain language, so nothing catches you out at the finish line.

The biggest pieceThe deposit: cash and CPF

The deposit is the chunk of the price you pay yourself, before the loan covers the rest. It comes from two pockets: actual cash in your bank account, and your CPF Ordinary Account (the savings set aside from your pay each month). How much you need, and how much of it must be cash rather than CPF, depends entirely on the kind of loan you take. That is the next piece.

What this means for you: before you fall for a flat, separate your savings into two numbers, cash and CPF. The cash number is usually the one that decides what is realistic.

It changes everythingHDB loan or bank loan

This single choice changes how much you need upfront and how it splits between cash and CPF.

  • An HDB loan lets you borrow a larger share of the price, so the deposit is smaller. Better still, you can pay that deposit using your CPF, with little or no cash required. The trade-off is a slightly higher interest rate, and you must meet HDB's eligibility and income rules.
  • A bank loan lends a smaller share, so the deposit is larger. A fixed slice of the price must be paid in actual cash, and it cannot come from CPF. The rest of the deposit can be CPF. Banks often start cheaper, but the rate can move over time.
What this means for you: an HDB loan is gentler on cash, a bank loan can be cheaper on interest but demands more cash upfront. The right one depends on how much cash you hold today. The afford tool shows both splits for your numbers.

A tax everyone paysBuyer's Stamp Duty

Buyer's Stamp Duty (BSD) is a government tax on the purchase, worked out on whichever is higher, the price you pay or the flat's valuation. Everyone pays it, and the rate steps up the more the home costs. Crucially, it is normally settled from cash or CPF on top of your deposit, so it is easy to forget when you are budgeting.

If this is not your first property, or you are a permanent resident, there can be an extra layer called Additional Buyer's Stamp Duty. For a first-time buyer of a single HDB flat, BSD is usually the only stamp duty that applies.

What this means for you: set aside money for stamp duty as a separate line, not part of your deposit. The afford tool works out your exact figure from the price.

The smaller costsOption fee, legal and valuation fees

A handful of smaller costs sit alongside the big ones:

  • The option fee. When you and the seller agree on a price, you pay a small option fee to receive the Option to Purchase, the short contract that reserves the flat for you while you finalise the rest. It counts towards your deposit later, it is not an extra cost on top.
  • Legal fees. A conveyancing lawyer handles the paperwork and the transfer. The fee is modest and can usually be paid from CPF.
  • Valuation fee. If you take a bank loan, the bank values the flat first, and you pay a small valuation fee for that report. An HDB loan uses HDB's own valuation process.

Watch this onePaying above the valuation

Your loan and your CPF only stretch up to the flat's official valuation. If you agree to pay more than the flat is valued at, that gap, often called cash-over-valuation, has to be paid in cash, on top of everything else. It is one of the most common reasons a budget that looked fine suddenly falls short.

What this means for you: check what similar flats actually sold for before you commit to a price. Value the flat first so you are not paying a premium in cash without knowing it.

The monthly sideWhat you can borrow has a ceiling

How much you can borrow is not just about the deposit you can scrape together. It is capped by your income, through two rules:

  • MSR, the Mortgage Servicing Ratio. For an HDB flat, your home loan repayment alone cannot exceed a set share of your gross monthly income. This is the cap that makes HDB budgets feel tighter than condo ones.
  • TDSR, the Total Debt Servicing Ratio. If you take a bank loan, there is a second cap: all your monthly debt repayments together, the home loan plus any car loan, credit cards and other instalments, cannot exceed a larger share of your income.

Banks also check that you could still afford the repayments if interest rates rose, so the loan they offer is deliberately on the safe side.

Clear other debts before you apply, and the same income can support a bigger home loan.
What this means for you: your real borrowing limit is the lowest of what the rules allow, what you have saved, and how much of that is cash. The afford tool checks all three at once.

The good newsGrants bring it down

If you qualify, CPF housing grants reduce what you need to find. They top up your purchase, which means a smaller loan and, for many first-time families, far less cash and CPF out of pocket. Eligibility depends on things like your household income, whether you are a first-time buyer, and the flat you choose, so the amount varies from buyer to buyer.

What this means for you: never judge affordability on the sticker price alone. Grants can change the picture significantly, so factor them in before you decide a flat is out of reach.

Putting it togetherSo, how much do you actually need?

Add up the pieces and you get the real answer: the deposit (cash plus CPF, split by your loan type), Buyer's Stamp Duty, the option fee, legal and valuation fees, any amount above valuation in cash, and a comfortable monthly repayment that sits inside the income caps, all reduced by any grants you qualify for. The figures move with prices and rules, which is exactly why it is worth getting yours worked out rather than relying on a rough rule of thumb.
