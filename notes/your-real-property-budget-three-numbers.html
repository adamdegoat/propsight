Your property budget has three parts. Most buyers only count one.

22 June 2026 · 1-min read · by PropSight

We've watched buyers spend weeks falling for a home they couldn't actually afford. The reason is almost always the same. They started scrolling listings before working out three numbers. Sort these out first, and every place you view is one you can genuinely buy.

First, the big one: what the bank will actually lend you. Your budget isn't just your savings. It's your savings plus whatever a bank is willing to lend. And that loan has a ceiling. The bank looks at how much of your monthly income already goes to paying off debt, then caps your loan from there. So every car loan, credit card balance or instalment you're carrying quietly shrinks what you can borrow. Clear those off first and you'll often find your budget grows on its own. Work out your max loan →

Next, the cash and CPF you'll need upfront. Your down payment comes from two pockets. One is cash, the other is your CPF (the savings set aside from your pay each month). With a bank loan, a portion has to be paid in cash. CPF can cover a good part of the rest, but not all of it. If your cash savings are on the thin side, this is usually the number that decides what's realistic for you.

Stamp duty and legal fees come out of your own pocket, not your loan.

And last, the costs that sit on top of the price. Stamp duty is a government charge worked out on whichever is higher, the price you pay or the home's valuation. It adds up faster than most people expect. Add legal fees and a sensible renovation budget, and the cash you need on day one is well above the down payment alone. Estimate your stamp duty →
